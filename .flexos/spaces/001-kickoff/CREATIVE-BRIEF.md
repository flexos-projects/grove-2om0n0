# Grove — Creative Brief

## The One Thing

This should make guests feel like the hotel already knows what they need — before they finish asking.

## Who This Is For (Real Description)

A guest at a 30-room hotel in Tulum or Marfa or Bath — someone who chose this place *because* it isn't a Marriott. They're lying by the pool and they want a dinner reservation at the place only locals know. They don't want to download an app. They don't want to walk to the front desk. They want to text a number and have it handled. They chose this hotel because it feels human, and the concierge should feel human too — even when it isn't.

## What This Is Not

- Not a chatbot. Chatbots feel like customer service. This feels like a person who works at the hotel.
- Not a hotel management system. The operator dashboard exists to serve the guest experience, not the other way around.
- Not trying to look like a tech product. No gradients, no glassmorphism, no startup energy. This is hospitality software that feels like hospitality.
- Not an app guests download. SMS is the interface. The "app" is for hotel staff and owners.
- Not generic. Every hotel that uses this should feel like the service is *theirs*, not a platform's.

## Personality (5 adjectives)

Discreet but present. Warm but never familiar. Competent but never robotic. Confident but never loud. Considered.

## Aesthetic Direction

The staff-facing product should feel like the lobby of the hotel it serves — calm, intentional, with nothing out of place. Think Aesop's retail stores meets a well-designed reservation book. Information density of Linear, but with the warmth of natural materials. Deep forest green as the anchor — not as an accent, as the identity. White space used the way a good hotel uses silence. Typography that feels editorial, not corporate.

## Colour Direction

Deep forest green that means old-growth canopy, not emerald jewelry. It should feel like something that has been there longer than you. The palette around it: warm stone, aged paper, charcoal for text. No bright accents — this product whispers. If there's a secondary colour, it's a muted gold that means "candlelight," not "premium badge."

## Typography Direction

Headings that feel like a boutique hotel's room number plate — serif with quiet authority, the kind of typeface that looks better at large sizes because it has details worth seeing. Body text that disappears — legible, warm, invisible. A font you'd find in a well-edited travel magazine, not a SaaS dashboard.

## The Competitive Gap

Hotel concierge tools are either: (1) enterprise platforms built for chains that feel like airline booking systems, or (2) WhatsApp-based hacks held together with spreadsheets. Nobody has built a concierge product that feels as considered as the boutique hotels it serves. The gap is taste. Every competitor prioritized features over feeling. A boutique hotel owner who cares deeply about the pillowcases on the bed has no software that meets their standard.

## Implied Features (from the brief alone)

- **SMS-first guest interaction** — guests text, things happen. No app download, no login.
- **Hotel operator dashboard** — staff see requests, respond, track, coordinate.
- **Request routing** — a dinner reservation goes to one person, a room issue goes to another.
- **Proactive messaging** — "Your airport transfer is confirmed for 3pm" without being asked to follow up.
- **Multi-property support** — boutique hotel groups run 3-8 properties.
- **White-label feel** — the guest should never know what platform powers this.
- **Conversation history** — guest texts again next visit, the hotel remembers their preferences.
- **Quick actions** — common requests (restaurant recs, late checkout, taxi) should be one-tap for staff.

## Open Questions

1. **AI or human behind the SMS?** "Butler in your pocket" could mean AI-powered responses with human escalation, or purely human staff using the tool to respond. This changes the entire technical architecture. I'm assuming hybrid — AI handles simple requests and drafts responses, staff approves or handles complex ones — because that's where the market is moving and it's the only way to offer 24/7 service at boutique scale.

2. **Who pays — the hotel or the guest?** I'm assuming the hotel subscribes (B2B SaaS), not the guest. This means the operator dashboard and onboarding are critical paths, even though the guest experience is the soul of the product.

3. **Integration depth** — does this connect to the hotel's PMS (property management system), or is it standalone? I'm assuming standalone-first with PMS integrations as a growth feature, because boutique hotels often use wildly different systems (or none at all).
